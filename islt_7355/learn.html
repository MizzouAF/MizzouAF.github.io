Observe With Us


Join me as we begin our journey into learning the basics of meteorological observation. We have broken observing into three lessons to help teach you how to combine each section together to form a meteorological observation!



Cloud Observing

Learning Goal: Using their visual senses and imagery or video students will be ble to differentiate between differing cloud types to be able to accurately describe the currently observed clouds they are looking at and how that cloud relates to the stability of the atmosphere.

Clouds give us an amazing understanding of the current stability of the atmosphere. Different cloud types mean the atmosphere is stable or unstable. This gives us a huge clue as to what kind of weather we can expect to see in the near future. In this section students will learn how to successfully analyze and explain the current nature of the atmosphere by determining which cloud type they are viewing.

Clouds are categorized by different types and even further categorized by their heigh in the atmosphere in which they reside. The image above and the video to the left from NOAA SciJinks help to explain these different cloud types and how they give a visual clue to the overall stability of the current atmosphere.

Precipitation Observing

Learning Goal: Using visual senses, imagery, or videos students will be able to understand the basic principles of precipitation observation and precipitation types.



Precipitation much like clouds also give meteorologists an understanding of the current stability of the atmosphere. The type of precipitation, it’s rate of fall, intensity, and form are all vital to understanding what the current conditions within the atmosphere are. Throughout this section students will learn how to successfully identify, explain, and measure rain and snow precipitation types and understand how precipittion correlates to the overall stability of Earth’s atmosphere.

When we think of precipitation we generally think of rain. Rain and snow are the precipitation types that as novice observers that we can easily measure. Precipitation type can help tell meteorlogists more about the current atmospheric environment, when this information is observed in correlation to our cloud type observation from above.

Images like the one above from The National Weather Service give us an idea of how the atmosphere will produce different types of precipitation. This gives meteorologists an idea of the temperature profile throughout the atmosphere and aids in observing overall atmospheric stability. However, the amount of precipitation that has occured is just as important to understanding the sky. The video to the left and below from Community Collaborative Rain, Hail, & Snow Network (CoCoRaHS) shows us how to use precipitation guages to record and report total rainfall and snowfall amounts.

Weather Instruments

Learning Goal: Utilizing pictures and video, students will be able to have a basic understanding of different weather instruments, and how they are used to collect non-physically sensed data to add information to meteorological observations.

Clouds and precipitation are not the only factors to clue meteorologists into what the current stability of the atmosphere looks like. Features such as barometric pressure, wind speeds and direction, temperature, dewpoint temperature and relative humidity all play a role in understanding the dynamics of atmospheric stability. Throughout this section, students will learn about basic weather instruments and how they are used to measure different aspects of presently observed weather. When used in conjunction with their knowledge learned from the previous sections above it should aid the student in painting an overall atmospheric picture.

Weather instruments come in all shapes and sizes. They can consist of large weather stations like the image above from Windy.app or as handheld meters like the one to the left as we can see from USFWS Pacific Southwest Region. Whatever the size of your weatherintstruments they are used to help meteorlogists observe weather features not so aptly described by physical senses alone. The basic weather instruments consist of the following:


Instrument Use
Barometer Measures atmospheric pressure
Thermometer Measures ambient air temperature
Hygrometer Measures relative humidity
Anemometer Measures wind speed
Wind Vane Measures wind direction


Most at home observers and meteorlogists out in the field will generally use some form of handheld meter like the Kestrel meter in the video below from the University of Waterloo. These handheld meters are usually small and contain all the necessary instruments in a small handheld digital package to help make observing weather data easy.
